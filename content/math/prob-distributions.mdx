<Callout type="info">Probability distributions describe how likely different outcomes are. From the ubiquitous Gaussian to the versatile Beta, distributions are the vocabulary of probabilistic modeling.</Callout>

## What Problem Does It Solve?

When we model real-world data, we need to specify what kind of randomness we expect. Different distributions capture different patterns: coin flips, counts, continuous measurements, proportions.

## Key Discrete Distributions

### Bernoulli

Single binary trial: $X \in \{0, 1\}$ with parameter $p$:

$$
P(X = 1) = p, \quad P(X = 0) = 1 - p, \quad E[X] = p, \quad \text{Var}(X) = p(1-p)
$$

### Binomial

Number of successes in $n$ independent Bernoulli trials:

$$
P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}, \quad E[X] = np
$$

### Poisson

Number of events in a fixed interval with rate $\lambda$:

$$
P(X = k) = \frac{\lambda^k e^{-\lambda}}{k!}, \quad E[X] = \text{Var}(X) = \lambda
$$

## Key Continuous Distributions

### Uniform

All values in $[a, b]$ equally likely:

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{b - a}, \quad E[X] = \frac{a+b}{2}
$$

### Gaussian (Normal)

The most important distribution in statistics and ML:

$$
f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
$$

$$
E[X] = \mu, \quad \text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2
$$

<Callout type="tip">The Gaussian appears everywhere because of the Central Limit Theorem: the sum of many independent random variables tends toward a Gaussian, regardless of their individual distributions.</Callout>

### Exponential

Time between events in a Poisson process:

$$
f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0, \quad E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}
$$

### Beta

Distribution over probabilities $p \in [0, 1]$ with parameters $\alpha, \beta > 0$:

$$
f(p) = \frac{p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha, \beta)}, \quad E[p] = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}
$$

## Multivariate Gaussian

For $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance $\Sigma$:

$$
f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2} |\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)
$$

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

# Gaussian
x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 100)
pdf = stats.norm.pdf(x, loc=0, scale=1)
print(f"P(-1 ≤ X ≤ 1) = {stats.norm.cdf(1) - stats.norm.cdf(-1):.4f}")  # ≈ 0.6827

# Beta distribution
alpha, beta = 2, 5
print(f"Beta({alpha},{beta}) mean = {alpha/(alpha+beta):.4f}")

# Multivariate Gaussian
mean = np.array([0, 0])
cov = np.array([[1, 0.5], [0.5, 1]])
samples = np.random.multivariate_normal(mean, cov, size=10000)
print(f"Sample covariance:\n{np.cov(samples.T).round(2)}")
```

## Key Takeaway

Distributions are the building blocks of probabilistic models. Every assumption about data generation, every prior belief, every noise model uses a distribution. Mastering the key distributions — Gaussian, Bernoulli, Beta, Poisson — is essential for Bayesian inference, generative models, and understanding uncertainty in AI.
