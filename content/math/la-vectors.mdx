<Callout type="info">Vectors are ordered arrays of numbers — the fundamental data structure for representing features, embeddings, and directions in machine learning.</Callout>

## What Problem Does It Solve?

A single number (scalar) can't describe a data point with multiple features. A house listing has price, square footage, number of bedrooms, location coordinates — all at once. Vectors bundle these features into a single mathematical object.

## Definition

A **vector** is an ordered list of $n$ real numbers:

$$
\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^n
$$

A vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ might represent a 3D point: $\mathbf{p} = [x, y, z]^T$.

## Vector Operations

### Addition

$$
\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\ a_2 + b_2 \\ \vdots \\ a_n + b_n \end{bmatrix}
$$

Geometrically, vector addition follows the **parallelogram rule**.

### Scalar Multiplication

$$
c \cdot \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} c \cdot v_1 \\ c \cdot v_2 \\ \vdots \\ c \cdot v_n \end{bmatrix}
$$

## Dot Product

The dot product (inner product) of two vectors produces a **scalar**:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} a_i b_i = a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + \cdots + a_n b_n
$$

Geometrically:

$$
\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = \|\mathbf{a}\| \|\mathbf{b}\| \cos(\theta)
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the vectors. Two vectors are **orthogonal** when $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0$.

<Callout type="tip">The dot product is the most used operation in neural networks. Every neuron computes a dot product of inputs and weights, then adds a bias.</Callout>

## Norm

The **norm** (length) of a vector:

$$
\|\mathbf{v}\|_p = \left( \sum_{i=1}^{n} |v_i|^p \right)^{1/p}
$$

Common norms:
- **L1 norm** (Manhattan): $\|\mathbf{v}\|_1 = \sum |v_i|$
- **L2 norm** (Euclidean): $\|\mathbf{v}\|_2 = \sqrt{\sum v_i^2}$
- **L∞ norm** (Max): $\|\mathbf{v}\|_\infty = \max_i |v_i|$

## Unit Vectors and Basis Vectors

A **unit vector** has norm 1: $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|}$

The **standard basis vectors** in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$
\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{e}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Any vector can be expressed as a linear combination of basis vectors:

$$
\mathbf{v} = v_1 \mathbf{e}_1 + v_2 \mathbf{e}_2 + v_3 \mathbf{e}_3
$$

```python
import numpy as np

a = np.array([1, 2, 3])
b = np.array([4, 5, 6])

# Vector addition
print(a + b)  # [5, 7, 9]

# Dot product
print(np.dot(a, b))  # 32

# Norms
print(np.linalg.norm(a, ord=2))  # Euclidean norm ≈ 3.742

# Unit vector
a_hat = a / np.linalg.norm(a)
print(np.linalg.norm(a_hat))  # 1.0
```

## Key Takeaway

Vectors are the workhorse of machine learning. Every data point is a vector, every layer in a neural network processes vectors, and every embedding is a vector. Mastering vector operations is essential for understanding AI.
