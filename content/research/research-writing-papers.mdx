## Paper Structure

### Abstract (150-300 words)

1. Problem statement (1-2 sentences)
2. Gap in existing work (1 sentence)
3. Your approach (1-2 sentences)
4. Key results (1-2 sentences)

### Introduction (1-2 pages)

1. **Motivation:** Why is this problem important?
2. **Background:** What existing work exists?
3. **Gap:** What is missing?
4. **Contribution:** What do you propose?
5. **Results:** What are your main findings?
6. **Organization:** How is the paper structured?

### Related Work (1-2 pages)

Group related work by theme, not chronologically. Show how your work differs from and builds upon prior art.

### Method (3-5 pages)

1. Problem formulation
2. Overview of your approach
3. Detailed description of each component
4. Training procedure
5. Implementation details

### Experiments (3-5 pages)

1. **Setup:** Datasets, metrics, baselines
2. **Main Results:** Comparison with baselines
3. **Analysis:** Why does it work?
4. **Ablation:** What matters?

### Conclusion (0.5-1 page)

1. Summary of contributions
2. Limitations
3. Future work

## Writing Tips

1. **Write the abstract last** - it summarizes the whole paper
2. **Use active voice** - "We propose" not "It is proposed"
3. **Be specific** - "Our method achieves 95.2%" not "Our method achieves high accuracy"
4. **Use figures** - They convey complex ideas quickly
5. **Cite properly** - Give credit where it's due

## Common Mistakes

1. Overselling contributions
2. Ignoring limitations
3. Poor figure quality
4. Missing important citations
5. Inconsistent notation

## Key Takeaways

1. Follow the standard structure (Abstract, Intro, Related, Method, Experiments, Conclusion)
2. Be clear, specific, and honest about limitations
3. Figures and tables communicate complex ideas effectively
4. Good writing takes multiple revisions
